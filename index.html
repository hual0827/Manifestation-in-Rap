Manifestation in Rap

Quantitative analysis on how rappers shape their own destiny

"To everyone who sell me drugs:
Don't mix it with that bullshit, I'm hopin' not to join the 27 Club"
- Mac Miller

Malcolm James McCormick, known professionally as Mac Miller, did not join the 27 Club. He was one year away, dying from a drug overdose at 26. About a month before his death, he released an album called Swimming in August.

One of the most talked about lines from the album is "That Mercedes drove me crazy, I was speedin'." How come that line got so much attention? Well, a few months before Swimming was released, he was arrested under suspicion of DUI after crashing his Mercedes into a power pole.

Many listeners assumed he wrote the song after crashing the car. However, here is a quote from Mac Miller in an interview with Craig Jenkins...

"Stuff like that always happens with my music. These themes will just connect because of, maybe, a state of mind I was in. [...] I’ll say a line like, “that Mercedes drove me crazy, I was speeding” I made that song way before the shit went down. You just have to get out of your own way, and everything will just connect for itself. [...] the story writes itself."

Even more recently, Jarad Anthony Higgins, AKA Juice WRLD, also died of an overdose. How come these rappers, loved by the world, are dying? As you will see in the dataset, Juice WRLD rapped heavily about drugs.

Maybe artists are just making art about what they already know. Maybe their rhymes are perpetuating their habits. Maybe being in the rap game pushes them to make explicit art, eventually manifesting in their real lives. Maybe all of the above. I'll let you decide, but I'll give you some numbers that might help.

I took 81 rappers, aggregated 35,000 lyrics from each rapper (drawing from their most popular albums) and counted the number of times they mentioned drugs using a simple keyword search... Does how often they mention drugs correlate to how they end up dying?

Deriving Meaning


Let's be honest, manifestation is more of a buzz word than an indisputable reason why we are seeing this correlation between how often rappers rap about deadly words and how they end up dying. It makes sense that people make art about what they already know and what they are exposed to regularly. It also makes sense that making art glorifying such negative topics perpetuates the same behavior.

Personally, I think it is pointless to investigate what came first between the environment or the art. Rather, we should make an effort to conciously notice what we are giving our attention to and how that is translating into our everyday lives.

Reflection Questions

  • Can other people's art translate into my own life as I expose myself to it?
